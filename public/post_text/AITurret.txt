<b>Problem Statement</b>:
dfgdfgdUnfortuantely, my family's birdfeeder has always been riddled with the occassional rodent jumping up 
and enjoying themselves with the seeds intented for aviators. Although we had tested previous traps, 
such as spring-based mechanisms, water-induced drowning systems, and various alike, a more 
curious approach is needed to ensure pests don't have the chance of perching themselves on our
birdfeeder right as we were watching.

<b>Mission</b>:


<b>Requirements</b>:
- A weapon strong enought to launch a projectile sufficient to deter or kill rats.
- A system by which the orientation of this weapon can be changed.
- A detection system capable of locating and tracking the rat in the vecinity around or
  directly on the birdfeeder.
- High enough accuracy to hit the supposed rodent from five meters away with >50% accuracy.
- Mountable and balanced interface with a grounded deck or a chair.


<b>Prototype</b>:
I started by mocking up a small prototype of the turret, placing servo motors for yaw and pitch 
control. This setup, although fairly primative, let me experiment with various microcontrollers
like the ESP32 and the Raspberry PI for Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) tracking and servo control.

The original ESP32 used a camera to take pictures, relaying the information over a WIFI Access point
to my desktop computer where the images would be processed via the <a href="https://yolov8.com/">YOLO algorithm</a>.
After the computer located the target object, the cartesian coordinates were relayed back to the ESP32
where the associated yaw/pitch movements would reangle the weapon to point at the located object. 

Although this setup provided a low-cost, low-weight solution, I had to sacrifice a significant amount of
time to send the captured images to the computer and and recieve coordinates, making the implementation
too slow for most rat-shooting applications.

Fortunately, I had a Raspberry PI 4 on hand, allowing me to run the YOLO CNN inference on the turret itself
and save the expensive computer delay. Seeing that the Raspberry PI 4 has a significantly larger form factor,
and the servos on the preliminary prototype model lacked the custom PID loop nescessary to control a large
gun weight without jittering uncontrollably, I decided to redesign the turret with custom parts and stepper
motors. 

<b>MK1 CAD/3D Printed Model</b>:
Rather than throwing together a second metal prototype for the stepper motor setup, I decided to CAD the
unique belt-drive yaw and pitch mechanisms and 3D print them. 

Here is a picture of the 3D printed turret bearing. Due to the large amount of force exerted downards, it 
is best to use a tank-style bearing to spread the weight out and avoid breaking the shaft.

Keyed shafts are used to ensure the pulleys can be printed seperately and rotationally fixed during the
process of assembly.
