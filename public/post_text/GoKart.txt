<b>Problem Statement</b>:
As with most 12-year-olds trapped at home and incapable of seeing their friends, the pandemic was incredibly boring for me. Thankfully, I had already developed an interest in engineering and became fascinated with car repair, electronics, and petrol engines.

<b>Mission</b>:
With youthful ambition and the desire to start a project capable of assisting in future builds, I began the process of constructing a small, two-seated vehicle capable of drive and neutral gears, along with a steering shaft.

<b>Requirements</b>:

Convert a vertically mounted lawnmower engine into a horizontal-shaft motor capable of driving the vehicle’s rear axle.
Create a steering assembly capable of rotating both front wheels with relative ease and minimal mechanical play.
Build a frame strong enough to support my 100-pound body (this would change in the future!).
Develop a reliable belt-driven linkage between the motor and the rear axle, allowing the driver to adjust tension to engage and disengage the clutch.

(NOTE: I started this project five years ago, so my recollection may not be perfect.)

<b>Initial Design</b>:
I began building the frame of the mini vehicle by assembling two chassis side beams and a rear mount for the engine, which I had relocated from an old lawnmower.

All internal combustion engines require sufficient lubrication. Most yard mowers use a small pump to ensure oil travels through the crankshaft, piston rings, and other moving components. This system would normally suffice, but since the vehicle required a horizontally mounted crankshaft, I needed to redesign the oil delivery system.

I started by filing down the cast iron sleeve around the crankshaft and removing the engine’s front and rear oil seals to make this modification. I also used heat-resistant epoxy to fill old channels and ensure oil did not escape the newly oriented motor. Furthermore, I added a crankshaft “scoop” (a small protruding aluminum piece) to ensure oil in the sump would be picked up and splashed against the piston walls to prevent scoring.

<b>MK1 (Early Pandemic)</b>
Here was one of the first versions of the project. I was around 12 years old at the time, so my knowledge of mechanical engineering was not yet refined.

<img src="/post_images/GoKart/DF5A1F40-6D7E-4E73-AE01-3CC62F2C8324_1_105_c.jpeg" alt="Description of the image" style="width: 100%; height: auto;">

Starting from the front, a simple aluminum steering column, controlled by an offset steering shaft arm joint, moved the front wheels from side to side. Although this iteration was not sufficiently geared down or securely mounted, it was still able to drive.

Moving to the rear, the improvised lawnmower engine shaft transmitted rotational power through a pulley reduction system to the rear axle. As expected, a significant amount of force was required to put the vehicle into gear—pulling the lever connected to a pulley tightly against the belt so the engine could transfer energy to the drivetrain.

<img src="/post_images/GoKart/365D61B6-B9C5-4BDC-B8DF-397F389FFD85_1_105_c.jpeg" alt="Description of the image" style="width: 100%; height: auto;">

This first version, although extremely primitive and simplified, successfully got things moving (pun intended).

<b>Later Frame Adjustments and Engine Replacement</b>:

The frame was later updated with additional beams to support a two-person load. The engine was upgraded to a high-torque commercial motor, and the transmission was converted to a chain drive with an adjustable tensioner.

<img src="/post_images/GoKart/A93EB089-B173-499E-90A0-71CA04506A58_1_105_c.jpeg" alt="Description of the image" style="width: 100%; height: auto;">

A front bumper was added, along with a redesigned steering column—this time using steel instead of aluminum to better handle stress.

<img src="/post_images/GoKart/2F3643C9-578C-416E-B58A-F3C1D5FE2150_1_105_c.jpeg" alt="Description of the image" style="width: 100%; height: auto;">

An adjustable tensioner was mounted near the engine, allowing for easier modifications.
